Define

We begin by running exercises to figure out what and how we want to learn. It's important to re-evaluate what you want to learn every couple weeks.

Definition worked a couple different ways. The first way was to simply ask our Product Owner, Mike, what he wanted to learn. Then, we asked him to prioritize those learnings by most important. The second way was to run an experiment workshop. We set a timer and ran a few rounds of writing. The three rounds are:

  • What we've learned (5 minutes) // Discuss and group (15-20 minutes)
  • What we want to learn (5 minutes) // Discuss and group (15-20 minutes)

    • Choose top three things the team (or Product Owner) wants to learn and write:

  • How we might learn (5 minutes) // Discuss and group (15-20 minutes)

Now, you have an experiment backlog!


Plan

After we've prioritized what we want to learn, we write an experiment plan for how we'll learn it. Experiment plans must be testable and as small as possible.

Start with the hypothesis - what are you testing? Read it and ask yourself if you can prove it has succeeded or failed. We've added a "What we want to learn" section to flesh out the hypothesis or say it in a more human way. The sections of the experiment plan are:

  • Hypothesis
  • What we want to learn
  • Audience
  • Method
  • Success Metrics (both Quantitative and Qualitative)
  • Results (filled in after experiment is run)
  • Next Steps (filled out after generating insights)

We write our experiment plans in Hackpad and link them to Pivotal Tracker releases.


Build

Building experiments means running fast and ignoring elements that don't aid your test.

Building experiments means running fast and ignoring elements that don't aid your test. We've been using InVision to make quick, clickable prototypes with "just enough design" for testing. Luckily, we have an existing style guide to run off of and we aim to veer from it only when it's important for our test. Challenge yourself (and your team) to build less software and manually support experiments. How might you concierge this test? What's most complex about the test? Could we reduce the work needed to achieve the same learning? The faster a test gets out, the faster you can learn. That being said - if something is legitimately easier to just build, don't hold back. We're trying to reduce waste, not stop development.
